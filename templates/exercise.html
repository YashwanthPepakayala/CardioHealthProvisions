3 Exercises for a Healthy Heart

Join a group of seniors practicing yoga in the park for improved heart health. Experts recommend three types of exercises to boost your cardiovascular health and overall well-being.




Aerobic Exercises

Aerobic exercise involves moving your body's largest muscles, like those in your arms and legs, in a way that makes you warm and slightly out of breath. Examples include walking, cycling, swimming, and even everyday activities like housework, gardening, or playing with children.

Aerobic exercise helps your heart and circulatory system work better, making daily tasks easier without leaving you tired or breathless. It also improves emotional well-being and sleep quality.

It's recommended to aim for at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise. Start gradually if you're new to exercise, building up from 5 to 10 minutes daily, and listen to your body.

Everyone with a heart or circulatory condition can benefit from aerobic exercise, but it's important to adjust the intensity based on your condition and fitness level. Consult with your healthcare professional before starting any new exercise regimen.





Strength Training

Perform bodyweight exercises or use resistance bands to build muscle strength. Strong muscles support your cardiovascular system, enhance mobility, and reduce the risk of injury.

Strengthening your muscles reduces the strain on your heart and makes daily activities easier. Stronger muscles also improve your ability to perform aerobic exercises without getting too tired or breathless.

Try to incorporate resistance training into your routine two to three days a week, with at least a day of rest in between sessions. Start with exercises you can comfortably repeat 10 to 12 times, gradually increasing the challenge. Aim for two to three sets of each exercise.

Everyone can benefit from some form of strength training. However, if you have specific heart conditions or have recently undergone surgery, it's essential to consult with your healthcare professional before starting.



Balance and Flexibility Exercises

Balance and flexibility exercises, like yoga, pilates, and tai chi, improve your ability to move smoothly and stay steady on your feet.

Flexibility exercises prevent muscles from becoming too tight, helping you move more freely and reducing the risk of pain or injury.

Balance exercises decrease the likelihood of falls.

Aim for two to three sessions of balance and flexibility exercises per week. Start gradually and hold stretches for at least 30 seconds, focusing on a "comfortable tightness" rather than pain. Remember to breathe normally during stretches.

Everyone, including those with heart or circulatory conditions, can benefit from balance and flexibility exercises. However, individuals on blood pressure medication should take extra care and move slowly when changing positions to prevent dizziness.

Expert Advice

Helen Alexander, a physiotherapist specializing in cardiac rehabilitation, emphasizes the importance of tailored exercise plans. Regardless of your age or existing heart condition, staying active can alleviate symptoms, enhance daily functioning, and prevent further deterioration.
